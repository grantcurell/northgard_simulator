Yes, but not “all the game rules.” What your developer needs is a mathematically faithful model of the subset of Northgard that can change Lynx combat power at 20:00. For this project, the simulator’s job is to transform a game state at time 0 into a game state at time 20:00, then score the resulting army. The minimum faithful state is: time, season, resources, population, happiness, housing cap, zones owned, zone graph, buildings, unit roster, warband cap, lore unlocked, fame, hunting trophies, forge state, and all Lynx-specific unlocks. Villagers only spawn while happiness is at least 0, their spawn speed depends on happiness and current population, the Town Hall starts with housing capacity 6 and generates +3 kröwns, and Houses cost 50 wood and 10 kröwns. Military units are mostly villager conversions that require warband space and continue consuming food and wood while producing nothing. ([Northgard Wiki][1])

Tell him to build it as an event-driven simulator, not a frame-by-frame clone. Northgard’s relevant math is mostly discrete events: villager spawn, colonization, building started, building completed, lore unlocked, trophy threshold hit, camp upgrade finished, weapon forged, unit recruited, neutral tile cleared. Every action should be a state transition with a start time, completion time, resource delta, and assignment delta. The optimizer can then search over legal actions, but the simulator itself should remain deterministic and table-driven. That matters because many of the biggest game effects here are additive thresholds and cost ladders, not continuous physics. ([Northgard Wiki][2])

For economy, model every resource as a stock-flow equation. At any instant, each resource is:

R(t + dt) = R(t) + production(t) * dt - consumption(t) * dt - instant_costs(t).

Food, wood, kröwns, stone, iron, lore, fame, and hunting trophies are the relevant tracked currencies. Villagers gather 4 food in summer and 2.4 food in winter when unassigned. Town Hall gives +3 kröwns. Merchants produce 2 kröwns each. Marketplace buy prices are 2 per food, 3 per wood, 40 per stone, and 50 per iron, with a shared refill pool. Buildings cost wood and/or kröwns and usually impose annual kröwn upkeep, so your code should normalize all rates to one unit, ideally per second or per month. ([Northgard Wiki][3])

For population, the model should separate population count, housing capacity, and happiness. Housing capacity at minimum starts at 6 from Town Hall; each House costs 50 wood and 10 kröwns, and an upgraded House adds +3 extra population capacity and removes the “better houses” happiness penalty. Town Hall upgrade adds +20% population growth and +2 population cap. Happiness at 0 or above allows villager spawning; more happiness means faster spawning; larger total population slows the spawn speed. Recruitment adds a permanent +25% population growth speed, plus a temporary +5% for 4 months per clan unit killed by an enemy or sacrificed, up to +75%. Shiny Happy People reduces happiness required from population by 20%. Since the exact spawn-time formula is not exposed cleanly in the current official wiki, your developer should implement villager spawn with a parameterized function `spawn_interval = f(happiness, population, recruitment_bonus, town_hall_upgrade)` and calibrate it from recordings if exact replication matters. ([Northgard Wiki][1])

For map and expansion, treat Northgard as a graph of zones. Each zone needs: owner, neighbors, terrain tags, special resources, building-slot count, current buildings, neutral pack if any, and whether a lure is present. Colonization is a food cost paid to convert a neutral zone into your zone; the Lynx Colonization lore reduces that food cost by 30%. Your optimizer must model neutral clearing explicitly because early military investment can accelerate zone acquisition, and earlier zone acquisition changes food, wood, kröwns, happiness sources, and later military cap. Mielikki, Brundr, and Kaelinn also affect decolonization via Nature Takeover, so zone state is part of combat math too, not just macro. ([Northgard Wiki][4])

For buildings, treat every building as a typed object with build cost, build time, upkeep, worker slots, upgrade cost, and upgrade effect. The key Lynx military building is the Archery Range: it costs 50 wood, gives +2 warband, converts villagers into Trackers, and also recruits Mielikki, Brundr, and Kaelinn. Upgrading an Archery Range costs 100 wood, 50 kröwns, and 10 stone, adds +1 warband, and gives Trackers +5% attack power. Archery Mastery makes the first Tracker free and halves Archery Range upgrade cost. Forge costs 60 wood and 15 kröwns; its upgrade adds a second smith and +20% forging speed. Your simulator should therefore represent building choices as capital allocation problems with both capacity and multiplier effects. ([Northgard Wiki][5])

For unit recruitment, use conversion math rather than barracks-production math. Trackers are villager conversions that require warband space. Their base stats are 50 health, 10 attack, 3 defense, and their hiring cost scales linearly: the first costs 25 kröwns, then each additional Tracker costs 5 more, so the total cost for n Trackers is an arithmetic series. If you number Trackers from 1, then `cost_i = 20 + 5*i`, and total cost is `sum(cost_i, i=1..n) = 2.5*n^2 + 22.5*n`. The wiki gives the full ladder explicitly, for example total cost 340 for 8 and 475 for 10. Mielikki costs 150 kröwns and no iron. Brundr costs 60 kröwns + 40 food. Kaelinn costs 40 kröwns + 60 food. Because Trackers use warband but special units like Warchiefs do not consume upkeep or housing in the same way, keep their recruitment math separate. ([Northgard Wiki][6])

For Lynx-specific macro, there are four big systems to model: Hunting Trophies, Path of the Hunter, fame thresholds, and lures. Lynx earns Hunting Trophies on animal kills, and Path of the Hunter replaces the normal military paths. Trophy unlock costs are fixed by unlock number, not by branch: 30, 80, 130, 190, 250, 310, 380, 440, 510, 590, 660, and 740. That means the trophy optimization is a staircase problem, not a continuous one. Your code should track trophies as a stock and unlocked path nodes as a growing ordered set. ([Northgard Wiki][4])

The specific path nodes that matter to a 20-minute lethality model should be coded as exact multipliers or rule toggles. Caring Siblings makes Brundr and Kaelinn regenerate health on the same allied zone and gives Brundr +30% attack power while with Kaelinn. Sibling Rivalry makes both lynxes produce food in territory and gives Kaelinn +40% defense while with Brundr. Double Shot gives Trackers an extra arrow on nearby enemies for 30% damage. Wild Hunter removes supply malus and winter attack-power penalty for military units. Path Complete can be bought repeatedly and each purchase gives all Trackers +5% attack speed when they can attack from distance, +5% range, and +5% move speed. The Wise One makes Mielikki produce +3 lore outside combat in your territory. These are not flavor; they are major multiplicative terms in your combat evaluator. ([Northgard Wiki][2])

For fame, model at least the 200 and 500 breakpoints. At 200 fame Lynx unlocks Mythical Lure; once placed it periodically attracts mythical beasts, and each cleared wave increases the size of the next one. The next feast cost is reduced by 7.5% per animal your clan killed, up to 50%. At 500 fame Mielikki unlocks Oskoreia, which recalls the strongest mythical wave the clan has killed. Fame therefore acts as both an economy/combat unlock axis and an indirect function of how aggressively you farm animals. Your state machine should therefore include fame production and breakpoint-triggered mechanics, not just final fame count. ([Northgard Wiki][4])

For food infrastructure, Spoils of Plenty must be modeled as a discrete alternative to normal food buildings. It lets you place up to 2 Standard Lures in your territory to attract Savage Deer with +10% meat over normal deer. Villagers assigned there become Hunters with 10% bonus production; hunters’ tool improvement becomes free and their forging time is halved. Since this substitutes a lore plus lure-placement pipeline for part of the normal food-building pipeline, it changes both wood allocation and forge allocation. In code, model a lure as a zone-level food source with its own worker slots, production table, and prerequisite. ([Northgard Wiki][4])

For generic lore, the simulator needs only the nodes that can move the 20-minute optimum. Sharp Axes is +20% woodcutter production. Recruitment is +25% permanent pop growth. Hearthstone reduces extra winter firewood by 50% and winter food penalty by 20%. Weaponsmith gives +20% attack power to all military units. Feeling Safe gives +3 happiness if you have a Warchief and +1 happiness per upgraded military camp. Those are the biggest first-order levers on whether the optimizer prefers a greedier economy line, a bigger late conversion, or a smaller but stronger quality army. Model them as exact modifiers, not fuzzy heuristics. ([Northgard Wiki][4])

For winter and maintenance, keep the model simple but explicit. Villagers drop from 4 food in summer to 2.4 in winter. Hearthstone reduces winter food penalty by 20% and extra wood firewood use by 50%. Buildings and military units impose upkeep in kröwns or resources; if kröwn upkeep is unpaid, buildings take damage. Military units also keep consuming food and wood while not producing resources. For a 20-minute timing optimizer, this means “build army early” carries a real shadow cost in lost economic output and upkeep, so the simulator must reflect that continuously through time rather than only at recruit time. ([Northgard Wiki][3])

For combat, do not try to clone the hidden engine on day one. Make the combat layer a separate evaluator that consumes the 20:00 army state. Start with effective combat value per unit:

`ECV = HP * survivability_weight + DPS * uptime_weight + utility_bonus`.

Then apply modifiers from Weaponsmith, camp upgrades, trophy nodes, winter, supply, and adjacency conditions. For Trackers, include additive attack-power boosts from upgraded Archery Ranges, the extra-arrow expected value from Double Shot, and the repeated scaling from Path Complete. For Brundr and Kaelinn, condition their bonuses on being in the same zone. For Mielikki, include her support value through lore generation before combat and her 500-fame Oskoreia option after that threshold. Because some true engine formulas are not documented cleanly, your developer should architect combat coefficients as data-driven parameters that can later be fitted from test fights. That way the simulator is useful now and calibratable later. The documented pieces you can trust immediately are the unit stats and the named buffs. ([Northgard Wiki][6])
